[Break out the Breville, it&#8217;s time for a Toastie](http://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/wordofmouth/2014/mar/13/breville-toastie-toasted-cheese-sandwich) by John Naylor
  
Do you like toasted sandwiches? Apparently they&#8217;re making a comeback in restaurants and cafes. I&#8217;d like to contend they never left. Whenever we were unwell our mum would make us toasted sandwiches for lunch/dinner full of either creamed corn or Heinz tinned spaghetti. When I left home back in the 90&#8217;s my grandma gave me her toasted sandwich maker which looked like this one:

It made perfectly toasted sandwiches with lava like molten insides if you weren&#8217;t careful and you had to be careful not to let the bread packet or butter tub touch any exposed metal or you&#8217;d experience the acrid smell or burning plastic and a rather sorry looking bread bag. It&#8217;s been central in every food culture from kim chi to gravalax and it&#8217;s more perhaps that some people in the twenties and thirties are realising that their working parents were more inclined to buy a jar than spent time standing over a pot and are keen to learn to make their own.   <div style="background: white; mso-outline-level: 1;">
    I read this post with interest about the sticky issues of recipe ownership and copyright infringement when it comes to blogging. She (and other bloggers) have found themselves in a touch of contention with Dan Leppard and his agent for creating recipes which are derived from his books or credited to his work. I know in my experience that there are some recipes that can&#8217;t be owned per se because they&#8217;ve been passed down through so many hands. Like strawberry jam. I&#8221;m only aware of two different stove top versions and they don&#8217;t differ whatever book you read. But if you choose to add other ingredients, whether spices or liquers etc, then they become that of their creator in my humble opinion.
  
  
  <div style="background: white; mso-outline-level: 1;">
  


This differs from buying or downloading a free copy or a Jamie Oliver book. Sure, he has plenty of money, but less books bought mean less people employed all the way down the line. It&#8217;s very hard to make a profit in publishing cookbooks if you aren&#8217;t one of the big names. But I also think bloggers need to be a bit smarter. If you run a blog full of recipes then publish a book with the same recipes (and usually additions) and gain publicity and a good reputation, well no wonder people are passing around your original blog recipes! That said however, your original creations and building of your audience may what enabled you to get a book deal in the first place. What are your thoughts?
